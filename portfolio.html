Snake VR

Click to Play

Controls:
Start/Pause: Enter
Move snake up/down/left/right: I/J/K/L
Move snake back/forwards: G/B
Move camera (on keyboard): W/A/S/D + mouse
Move camera (on VR): move your head

Snake VR is a VR game proof-of-concept developed in A-Frame for Marc Downie's Virtual Reality Production course in Spring 2022. For this project, I recreated the classic phone game Snake in VR, with a twist: you collide with your tail not based on objective coordinates, but based on your perspective.

I used raycasting to achieve perspective-based collisions. I also included sound effects and a keyboard-based control system. I still plan to return to this project someday and implement controller support and better visual effects. A two-player competitive mode is also a stretch goal.

This is the only incomplete project in this portfolio. I included it to demonstrate my continued interest in programming and to show my proficiency with a variety of Javascript libraries beyond P5.js.

Harvest

Click to Play

Controls explained in game

Harvest is a web-based grid puzzle game in which players tend to a community garden and choose between supporting their artistic neighbourhood or reaping individual benefits. Players are at first encouraged to plant neat, segregated rows of purebred flowers, for which they receive awards and accolades from bureaucratic officials. But if they instead mix and match plants, they can create techno-plant crossbreeds that revitalize the community center and inspire its members to create together.

Harvest has an explicit political message: grassroots community arts organizations often suffer under the weight of bureaucratic grants and institutionalized arts creation pipelines. Often, artists must choose between serving their community and getting paid or recognized. We were inspired by real events: this game was created in the wake of the eviction of the Toronto Media Arts Centre, a collective of media and interactive arts organizations, from a downtown location they had occupied for over five years. The loss of TMAC has been felt intensely by the Toronto independent game dev community and we chose to express our anger and grief through creative work.

I worked as the lead designer and programmer for this project, completed in about a month between June and July 2021. I created this game with the Gamma Space co-op, a nascent game design co-operative, in collaboration with media artist Jordan Sook.

I programmed Harvest start to finish in P5.js, a Javascript library for creative coding. I optimized the game to work on both desktop (on Firefox, Google chrome and Safari) and mobile devices. I implemented a scalable system for narrative elements and plant combinations. I also programmed UI, sound and visual interactions in dialogue with a multi-disciplinary creative team.

The Faceless City

Click to Download

Controls:
Move: Mouse
Eat Face: Click + hold

The Faceless City is a single-player Flash-style minigame about a monster that eats faces. I created this game in April 2021 as part of the first Toronto Comic Arts Festival Comics X Games Jam, in collaboration with artist Erica Lee.

I programmed this game in the Godot engine and integrated mouse-based controls, a random villager generator, and arms with multiple joints. Unfortunately, the original project file with the source code has been lost, but you can download and play the game here (and perhaps extract the original GD files from the EXE & PCK included in the download).

Dear Lighthouse

Click to Play

Controls:
Click on objects to move to them.
Clickable objects: Flower, bed, refridgerator, medal, mail chute, stairs, lighthouse light

Dear Lighthouse is a point-and-click story game about taking care of a lighthouse and caring for people from afar. This was my submission to Jamdemic 2020, a month-long game jam based on itch.io that I organized in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. I wanted to make a game that reflected both the deep loneliness and the profound sense of care I felt for people in my community as we isolated ourselves from each other. The work of a lighthouse keeper, who takes a faraway post and rarely sees the results of their work, but who keeps people safe and comforted, was an apt metaphor.

I took the lead with initial conceptual work, programmed the game in P5.js, and drew all of the artwork. As this is a point-and-click game, I programmed a system of pathways for the character to follow. I also integrated animations, responsive sounds, and randomized story elements. Britt Willis did all of the writing, including a number of letters that the keeper receives randomly throughout the game. Andrew Tran designed the eerie soundscape.

The Final Frontier

Click to Play

Controls:
Move: Move the mouse to the borders of the canvas
Speed up: W

The Final Frontier is a randomly-generated space exploration widget, developed as the final project for an arts-focused programming course with Nick Montfort in Spring 2018. Explore a randomly-generated field of solar systems, complete with a mini-map and semi-accurate planetary orbits.

This was the first game-like project I programmed. Using P5.js's WebGL-based 3D module, I made nearly everything randomly-generated, from stars and their locations to the planets that orbit them.
